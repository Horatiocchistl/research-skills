# Research Synthesis Methods Reference

## Types of Synthesis

### Narrative Synthesis
- Textual description and integration of findings across studies
- Best for: heterogeneous sources that cannot be statistically combined
- Risk: susceptible to author bias in source weighting without explicit protocols

### Systematic Review
- Pre-registered protocol; comprehensive and reproducible search strategy
- Explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria applied consistently
- Quality assessment of each included source
- Best for: answering a specific, well-defined question with a substantial evidence base
- Standard reporting: PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses)

### Meta-Analysis
- Statistical pooling of quantitative results across studies
- Produces a single weighted effect estimate with confidence interval
- Requires: comparable outcome measures and adequate primary data
- Best for: RCTs and quantitative studies with similar designs and populations
- Assess heterogeneity (I²) before interpreting pooled estimate

### Scoping Review
- Maps the extent and nature of evidence without full quality appraisal
- Best for: new fields, broad questions, or when the landscape of evidence is unclear
- Does not produce a definitive answer — identifies what research exists and where gaps are

### Thematic / Qualitative Synthesis
- Identifies patterns of meaning across qualitative studies
- Methods include: meta-ethnography, thematic synthesis, framework synthesis
- Best for: qualitative studies (interviews, ethnographies, case studies)

## Attribution Standards by Citation Format

### APA 7th Edition (Web/Electronic Sources)
*Authority: apastyle.apa.org — Webpage & Website References*

**In-synthesis citation:**
- Author-date: (Smith, 2021) or Smith (2021) found that…
- Multiple authors, same point: (Jones, 2019; Lee et al., 2022)
- Direct quote: (Author, Year, p. X)

**Reference list entry (webpage):**
```
Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Website Name. URL
```

**Special cases:**
- No date: (n.d.) in place of year
- No author: Title replaces author at start of entry
- Author = site: omit site name field

### MLA 9th Edition (Electronic Sources)
*Authority: Purdue OWL — MLA Works Cited: Electronic Sources*

**In-synthesis citation:**
- Author-page: (Smith 44) or Smith argues that… (44)
- No page numbers (web source): (Smith) or Smith contends…

**Works Cited entry (webpage):**
```
Last, First. "Title of Page." Website Name, Day Mon. Year, URL.
```

**With database (two containers):**
```
Last, First. "Article Title." Journal Name, vol. X, no. Y, Year, pp. Z–Z. Database Name, DOI or URL.
```

**DOI preference:** When a DOI is available, always use it over a URL in MLA 9.  
Format: https://doi.org/10.XXXX/XXXXX

## Avoiding Synthesis Bias

| Bias | Description | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Publication bias | Positive results more likely published | Search grey literature, preprints, trial registries |
| Availability bias | Over-weighting easily found sources | Use systematic search with documented strategy |
| Confirmation bias | Weighting sources that match prior view | Pre-specify themes before reviewing evidence |
| Recency bias | Assuming newer = more correct | Evaluate each source on methodology, not date alone |
| Language bias | Only including English-language sources | Note language restriction as a limitation |
| Funding bias | Sources funded by interested parties over-represented | Note funder for each source; flag potential conflicts |

## Evidence Confidence Levels (Synthesis Output)

Consistent language for confidence across the synthesis document:

| Level | Evidence basis | Language to use |
|---|---|---|
| Well-established | Multiple independent high-quality sources; no contradiction | "Research consistently shows…" / "Evidence strongly supports…" |
| Supported | ≥2 credible sources; minor contradicting evidence | "Evidence suggests…" / "Several studies indicate…" |
| Emerging | 1–2 sources; early findings; small scale | "Preliminary findings suggest…" / "Early evidence indicates…" |
| Contested | Credible sources on both sides | "Evidence is mixed…" / "Research is divided on…" |
| Unclear | Insufficient quality evidence | "The evidence base is insufficient to conclude…" / "This question remains unanswered in the literature." |

Never use *well-established* for a single-source finding, regardless of source quality.

## PRISMA Checklist (Systematic Reviews — Abbreviated)

Key items to report when conducting or evaluating a systematic review:

- [ ] Pre-registered protocol (PROSPERO or equivalent)
- [ ] Eligibility criteria stated (population, intervention, comparator, outcome, study design)
- [ ] Information sources listed (databases searched, date range)
- [ ] Search strategy reproducible (full search string provided)
- [ ] Study selection process described (number screened, excluded, included)
- [ ] Risk of bias assessment tool named and applied
- [ ] Synthesis method described (narrative, meta-analysis, etc.)
- [ ] Certainty of evidence assessed (GRADE or equivalent)
- [ ] Limitations acknowledged

Full PRISMA checklist: prisma-statement.org
