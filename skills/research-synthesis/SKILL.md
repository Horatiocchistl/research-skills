# Research Synthesis

Combine multiple sources into coherent, well-attributed research output — with honest representation of agreement, disagreement, and gaps.

## Core Principles

1. **Attribute everything**: every factual claim in the synthesis must trace to a specific source. No unattributed assertions.
2. **Synthesize, don't summarize**: arrange findings by theme or question, not by source. Summary says "Source A says X; Source B says Y." Synthesis says "On this question, sources agree that X [A, B], though C and D dispute the mechanism."
3. **Represent conflict honestly**: where sources disagree, say so explicitly. Do not smooth over genuine disagreement to produce a false consensus.
4. **Weight by quality**: give more prominent treatment to stronger evidence. Flag where weaker sources are included and why.
5. **Maintain neutrality**: the synthesis is a map of the evidence, not an argument for a conclusion. State what the evidence shows; do not advocate.

---

## Phase 1 — Source Inventory

Before writing, catalogue every source:

| ID | Title | Author(s) | Year | Type | Credibility tier | Key finding(s) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| S1 | ... | ... | ... | ... | 1–5 | ... |

Flag any source at credibility tier 4 or 5 before proceeding. Lower-tier sources may be included only when explicitly justified (e.g., as the only available primary account).

---

## Phase 2 — Claim Extraction

For each source, extract the key claims or findings that are relevant to the research question. Tag every claim with its source ID.

Format:
```
[S1] Finding or claim as stated or paraphrased, in your own words.
[S2] Contradicting or supporting finding.
```

Note the evidence type behind each claim (primary data, secondary analysis, expert opinion, institutional report, etc.).

---

## Phase 3 — Thematic Grouping

Group claims by theme or sub-question — **not** by source. This is the defining move of synthesis.

1. Identify 3–7 themes or sub-questions that the research question naturally divides into.
2. Place each extracted claim under the theme it addresses.
3. Within each theme, sort claims into:
   - **Consensus**: claims where ≥2 independent sources agree
   - **Partial agreement**: sources agree on general direction but differ on specifics
   - **Disagreement**: sources take genuinely opposing positions
4. Identify **gaps**: sub-questions that the available sources do not adequately address.

---

## Phase 4 — Conflict Resolution

For each thematic disagreement:

1. Compare the methodological strength of the conflicting sources (use credibility tiers and evidence type).
2. Determine the likely cause of disagreement:
   - Different definitions of key terms
   - Different populations, time periods, or contexts studied
   - Different methodologies producing different results
   - Genuine empirical contradiction requiring further research
3. Report the disagreement and its likely cause. Do not pick a side unless one source's evidence is overwhelming (e.g., a systematic review vs. a single anecdotal report).

---

## Phase 5 — Drafting

Structure the synthesis as follows:

**Introduction**
- State the research question or topic
- State the scope (time range, source types included/excluded, number of sources)
- State the organizational principle (thematic, chronological, methodological)

**Thematic Sections** (one per major theme from Phase 3)
- Open sentence: what the body of evidence shows on this theme
- Support each point with source tags: [S1], [S2, S4]
- Note disagreements and their nature (use Phase 4 findings)
- Close each section with a confidence statement: how settled is this evidence?
  - *Well-established*: multiple independent sources agree, no significant contradiction
  - *Emerging*: limited sources, early findings only, more research needed
  - *Contested*: credible sources in genuine disagreement
  - *Unclear*: insufficient quality evidence to draw any conclusion

**Gaps and Limitations**
- What aspects of the research question the current body of evidence cannot answer
- Why those gaps exist (no studies conducted, methodological barriers, access limitations)
- What type of research would be needed to fill them

**Conclusion**
- Most reliable takeaways from the evidence, stated with appropriate confidence
- Do not claim more certainty than the evidence warrants
- Directions for further research

**Source List**
- Full citations for all sources, numbered to match tags
- Format in APA 7th or MLA 9th as specified:

*APA 7 format (web/electronic):*
```
Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Website Name. URL
```

*MLA 9 format (electronic):*
```
Last, First. "Title of Page." Website Name, Day Mon. Year, URL.
```

---

## Phase 6 — Integrity Check

Before finalizing:

1. **Attribution check**: verify every factual claim in the synthesis has a source tag.
2. **Accuracy check**: read each tagged claim back against the original source — does the source actually support the synthesis statement?
3. **Overstatement check**: does the conclusion claim more than the evidence supports?
4. **Suppression check**: have any genuine disagreements been omitted from the thematic sections?
5. **Gap check**: are the limitations section and gap identification honest about what the sources cannot answer?

Do not finalize the synthesis until all five checks are complete.

## Attribution Patterns

| Situation | Phrasing |
|---|---|
| Single source | "Smith (2021) found that…" or [S1] |
| Multiple sources, same finding | "Several studies confirm this [S1, S3, S5]." |
| Contested finding | "While Jones (2019) argues X [S2], Lee et al. (2022) found the opposite [S4], likely because…" |
| Single-source finding | "Preliminary evidence suggests X [S2], though this rests on a single study and should be treated as tentative." |
| No source available | Never assert without a source — note the gap instead |

---

## Confidence Statement Guide

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| *Well-established* | Multiple independent, high-quality sources agree; no significant contradicting evidence |
| *Supported* | At least two credible sources agree; minor contradicting evidence |
| *Emerging* | One or two sources, early or small-scale; promising but not yet confirmed |
| *Contested* | Credible sources on both sides of genuine disagreement |
| *Unclear* | Insufficient quality evidence to draw a conclusion |

Use these terms consistently across thematic sections so the reader understands the relative confidence level of each part of the synthesis.

---

## Troubleshooting

- If sources use different definitions for a key term, define the term explicitly in the introduction and note how each source operationalizes it — this is often the root cause of apparent disagreement.
- If you have only one source on a sub-topic, report it but label it explicitly as a single-source finding.
- If sources are all from the same institution or funding source, note this as a limitation of the evidence base.
- Never present a contested finding as settled, even if the majority of your sources agree — note the dissenting evidence.
