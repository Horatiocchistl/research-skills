# Source Credibility Frameworks Reference

## CRAAP Test
**Origin:** Sarah Blakeslee, Meriam Library, California State University, Chico (2004)  
**Widely adopted in:** academic library instruction worldwide

Five dimensions: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose  
Scored 1–5 per dimension; total 25 points

## SIFT Framework
**Origin:** Mike Caulfield, University of Washington (2019); expanded in *Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers*

Four moves:
1. **Stop** — pause before sharing or citing; notice your emotional reaction
2. **Investigate the source** — lateral read before reading deeply
3. **Find better coverage** — look for the best available evidence, not just confirmation
4. **Trace claims** — find the original source of a statistic or quote; do not cite a citation

## RADAR Framework
**Origin:** Radford University Library

- **R**ationale: Why does this source exist?
- **A**uthority: Who created it and what are their credentials?
- **D**ate: When was it created or last updated?
- **A**ccuracy: Is it correct and supported by evidence?
- **R**elevance: Does it meet the specific research need?

## Lateral Reading
**Origin:** Stanford History Education Group (SHEG); Center for an Informed Public, University of Washington

Core principle: Expert fact-checkers leave a page quickly to find out about a source rather than reading it deeply first.

Three core questions:
1. Who is behind this information?
2. What evidence do they provide?
3. What do other sources say about this source?

## Publisher Credibility Tiers

| Tier | Examples | Research weight |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Highest** | Nature, Science, NEJM, The Lancet, peer-reviewed journals in any field | Full citation |
| **2 — High** | University press books, government statistical agencies (BLS, ONS, Eurostat, CDC, WHO) | Full — verify methodology |
| **3 — Moderate** | AP, Reuters, BBC, The New York Times, The Guardian, WSJ (news), professional associations | Corroborate for technical claims |
| **4 — Low** | Advocacy organizations, trade press, industry reports | Background; always corroborate |
| **5 — Minimal** | Anonymous sources, social media posts, AI-generated content without independent verification | Do not cite in research |

## APA 7 vs MLA 9: Source Quality Signals

Both APA 7 (apastyle.apa.org) and MLA 9 (Purdue OWL) require:
- Identifiable authorship
- Verifiable publication context
- Stable URL or DOI

A source that cannot satisfy these requirements for either standard should not be used as a primary research citation, regardless of its content quality.

**APA 7 signals of quality:**
- Author credentials in byline
- Date of publication (not just "last updated")
- Institutional affiliation for the publishing body
- DOI preferred over URL

**MLA 9 signals of quality:**
- Named author or clearly identified organizational author
- Quotable title (specific enough to be placed in quotation marks)
- Named container (site or database title) that can be italicized
- Stable permalink or DOI preferred over bare URL

## Social Media Credibility Notes

| Platform | Maximum credibility rating | Citability |
|---|---|---|
| Peer-verified account (government, major institution) | Moderate | APA7: yes (as social media post) / MLA9: yes (as tweet/post) |
| Verified individual expert | Low-moderate | With corroboration only |
| Unverified account | Minimal | Not for research |
| Anonymous or pseudonymous | None | Do not cite |

Social media citations in APA 7 and MLA 9:
- APA 7: Author [@handle]. (Year, Month Day). First 20 words [Type]. Platform. URL
- MLA 9: Last, First [@handle]. "Full text." *Platform*, Day Mon. Year, URL.
