# Peer Review

Provide structured, unbiased review of research content. The goal is to evaluate the work's quality and integrity — not to agree or disagree with its conclusions.

## Core Principles

1. **Neutrality before content**: evaluate how well the work is done before evaluating what it says. A well-executed study with unwelcome findings deserves a favorable methodological review. A poorly executed study with welcome findings deserves a critical one.

2. **Specificity always**: every critique and every commendation must cite a specific passage, section, table, or claim. Vague observations ("the writing is unclear") are not useful. Locate the problem.

3. **Constructiveness**: for every major flaw, offer a concrete, actionable remedy — not just a diagnosis.

4. **Completeness**: a review that finds only problems is as unreliable as one that finds only strengths. Acknowledge both.

5. **Explicit bias check**: before finalizing any review, audit your own evaluation for the four most common reviewer biases. Document this audit in the output.

---

## Phase 1 — Orientation

Read the entire work before writing a single evaluation note.

1. Identify: discipline, research type (empirical / theoretical / systematic review / case study / opinion / policy brief), stated purpose, and target audience.
2. Write down the author's stated thesis or research question in your own words.
3. Note the evidence type(s) used: quantitative data, qualitative data, archival, literature synthesis, argument.
4. Do not begin Phase 2 until the full work has been read.

## Phase 2 — Systematic Evaluation

Assess each dimension independently. Score each 1–5 (1 = major problem, 5 = excellent). Cite specific locations.

### A. Research Question / Thesis (weight: 20%)
- Is it clearly and explicitly stated?
- Is it appropriately scoped — not too broad to answer, not trivially narrow?
- Is the contribution novel, or does it replicate existing work without acknowledged justification?

## Phase 3 — Bias Audit

Before writing the formal review output, explicitly check for the following:

**1. Confirmation bias**  
Did I evaluate evidence differently based on whether the findings match my prior beliefs?  
Check: Did I give weaker evidence a pass because it supports a view I find plausible?

**2. Prestige bias**  
Did the author's institution, reputation, or affiliation influence my reading?  
Check: Would I evaluate this exact methodology differently if it came from a less prominent institution?

**3. Novelty bias**  
Did I rate the work higher simply because the findings are surprising or counterintuitive?  
Check: Is the novelty a function of genuine new evidence, or just an unexpected conclusion?

**4. In-group bias**  
Do I share a disciplinary, ideological, or institutional affiliation with the author that creates a blind spot?  
Check: Am I more forgiving of methodological shortcuts I also use?

Write a one-sentence bias audit statement confirming this check was performed and noting any bias identified.

## Phase 4 — Formal Review Output

Structure the review exactly as follows:

---

**REVIEW**

**Work reviewed:** [Title, Author(s), Date, Type]

**Summary** *(2–3 sentences)*  
State what the work does and what it attempts to show. Do not evaluate here — describe.

**Strengths** *(bulleted, minimum two)*  
Each bullet: what the strength is + the specific passage or section that demonstrates it.

**Major Concerns** *(numbered)*  
Each numbered item follows this structure:
> **Location**: [section/page/paragraph]  
> **Issue**: [what the specific problem is]  
> **Impact**: [why this matters to the work's validity or conclusions]  
> **Remedy**: [concrete action the author could take to address it]

Major concerns = issues that prevent acceptance without revision.

**Minor Concerns** *(bulleted)*  
Issues that should be addressed but are not blockers — clarity problems, citation gaps, minor inconsistencies.

**Recommendation**  
Choose one: **Accept** | **Minor Revision** | **Major Revision** | **Reject**  
One sentence rationale.

**Bias Audit**  
One sentence confirming the four-bias self-check was completed, and noting any identified.

---

## Recommendation Thresholds

| Recommendation | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Accept** | No major concerns; minor concerns only; contribution is clear and valid |
| **Minor Revision** | 1–2 addressable major concerns; core methodology is sound; thesis survives |
| **Major Revision** | Significant methodological or evidential gaps requiring new work; thesis may be salvageable |
| **Reject** | Thesis unsupported by evidence; methodology invalid; or work duplicates existing research without contribution |

## Troubleshooting

- If you lack domain expertise to evaluate a specific methodology, say so explicitly. Note which sections require specialist review and why, rather than guessing.
- For opinion essays (not empirical research), weight Phases 2E and 2F more heavily and skip or reduce 2C and 2D.
- If the work is a systematic review, apply PRISMA checklist criteria to Phase 2C/D in addition to the standard rubric.
