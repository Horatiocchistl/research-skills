# Peer Review Rubric & Reference

## Scoring Guide

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 5 | Excellent — meets or exceeds field standards |
| 4 | Good — minor issues, no significant impact on conclusions |
| 3 | Adequate — noticeable gaps; conclusions should be read with this in mind |
| 2 | Weak — significant problem that limits the work's reliability |
| 1 | Major flaw — conclusion is not supportable as written |

## Weighted Score Interpretation

| Weighted total | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| 4.5–5.0 | Accept |
| 3.5–4.4 | Minor Revision |
| 2.5–3.4 | Major Revision |
| Below 2.5 | Reject |

Use judgment: a score of 2 on Methodology (weight 25%) may alone warrant Major Revision regardless of overall score.

## Common Methodological Flaws by Research Type

### Empirical / Quantitative
- Sample too small to detect a meaningful effect (underpowered)
- No control group or inadequate comparison condition
- Confounding variables identified but not controlled
- p-value misinterpreted as measure of effect size or practical importance
- Selective reporting of outcomes (reporting only significant results)
- Regression to the mean not accounted for
- HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known) — presenting post-hoc analysis as pre-planned

### Qualitative
- Sampling strategy insufficiently described or unjustified
- No member-checking, triangulation, or negative case analysis
- Researcher positionality and potential bias not addressed
- Claims not grounded in direct quotations from data
- Saturation not demonstrated or discussed

### Systematic Review / Meta-Analysis
- Inclusion/exclusion criteria not pre-registered or inconsistently applied
- No assessment of individual source quality (risk of bias)
- Publication bias not addressed (funnel plot, Egger's test, grey literature search)
- Search strategy not reproducible (databases searched, date range, search terms not listed)
- Heterogeneity of included studies not assessed before pooling

### Opinion / Essay / Policy Brief
- Claims stated as facts without citation
- Only one side of a contested debate represented
- Counterarguments dismissed or strawmanned rather than engaged
- Distinction between empirical claims and value judgments not maintained

## Logical Fallacy Reference

| Fallacy | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **False dichotomy** | Only two options presented when more exist | "Either you support this policy or you support harm" |
| **Straw man** | Misrepresenting a counterargument to make it easier to refute | "Critics think we should do nothing" (when critics have a different proposal) |
| **Appeal to authority** | Citing credentials instead of evidence | "Expert X says so, therefore it's true" |
| **Correlation as causation** | Assuming shared timing or co-occurrence proves causation | "Crime rose after policy Y, so Y caused crime" |
| **Anecdote as data** | Using one case to generalize to a population | "I know someone who…" |
| **Circular reasoning** | Conclusion restates the premise | "It's true because the source says it's true" |
| **Slippery slope** | Assuming one step inevitably leads to extreme outcomes without evidence | "If we allow X, eventually Z will happen" |
| **Ad hominem** | Attacking the arguer rather than the argument | "The researcher has a conflict of interest, so the study is wrong" |

## Bias Audit Checklist (Phase 3 Expansion)

For each of the four biases, answer yes/no and note your reasoning:

**Confirmation bias**
- [ ] Did I apply the same skepticism to evidence that supports the thesis as to evidence that undermines it?
- [ ] Did I overlook a methodological flaw because I agree with the conclusion?

**Prestige bias**
- [ ] Would I rate this methodology the same if it came from a less prominent author or institution?
- [ ] Did the journal name or affiliation influence my reading before I assessed the content?

**Novelty bias**
- [ ] Is the novelty of the finding a reason I rated it higher?
- [ ] Is the surprising finding supported by proportionally strong evidence?

**In-group bias**
- [ ] Do I share disciplinary assumptions with this author that make me less likely to question their approach?
- [ ] Am I more lenient on shortcuts that are common in my own area of expertise?
